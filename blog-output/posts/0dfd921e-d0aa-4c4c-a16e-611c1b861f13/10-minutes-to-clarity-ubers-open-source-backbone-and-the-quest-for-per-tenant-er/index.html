10 Minutes to Clarity: Uber’s Open-Source Backbone and the Quest for Per-Tenant Error Counts

Unix intermediate unix 2 min read May 1, 2026

In Uber's world, metrics exploded across thousands of microservices, and Prometheus alone couldn’t scale to keep up 1. That reality spurred the move to a scalable, multi-region time-series store to power Prometheus at enterprise scale, shaping how teams think about observability 1. This story follows a deceptively simple problem—counting ERROR events per tenant and service from rotating logs—in a journey that reveals how a streaming mindset unlocks massive gains.

Hooking the World: Uber’s Wake-Up Call

Uber’s scale forced a rethink: a single Prometheus instance could not capture the pulse of thousands of microservices across regions 1. Building M3 changed the game, proving that a purpose-built backend could power Prometheus at enterprise scale. Building on this, the rest of the narrative treats a tiny but mighty question as a gateway to a broader architectural pattern: how to derive rolling, per-minute counts from a living fleet of rotating logs.

Journey: From Text Logs to JSON and Back

Many developers discover that production logs come in two forms: plain text lines like 'YYYY-MM-DD HH:MM:SS LEVEL: text' and JSON entries with fields ts, lvl, msg, tenant, and service 12. This mix demands robust parsing that respects both formats while ignoring unreadable files. The goal is a rolling 10-minute view, grouped by tenant and service, emitted as time marches forward and logs rotate.

Twist: The Right Toolset for the Job

The reliable path uses standard UNIX tools, stitched into a streaming pipeline: decompress gzipped archives with zcat, read plain logs with cat, and perform multi-key aggregation in a single awk-based pass. This approach leans on the broader ecosystem of time-series storage and scalable backends (M3 2, Cortex 11) to handle scale beyond a single host, while staying true to the constraint of using only built-in tooling for the core counting logic. The concept of sliding windows underpins the rolling 10-minute counts, enabling near-real-time insight as data flows in 5.

Real-World Proof

The Uber case study illustrates the need for an observable backend that scales with growth, while preserving Prometheus compatibility. That balance—open, scalable backends that augment Prometheus—has guided many teams toward architectures like M3 and Cortex to handle large, multi-region workloads 111. Such deployments reveal why organizations invest in time-series backends early, especially when logs and metrics grow faster than anticipated.

Payoff: How to Apply This in Your Environment

Start with a streaming mindset: treat the last-10-min window as a moving target, and align the pipeline to extract tenant and service from the log path (and from JSON fields when present). A robust, minimal approach can be sketched with a single pipeline that: (1) reads both gz and plain logs, (2) extracts metadata, (3) filters for ERROR-level events, (4) aggregates by tenant/service in 1-minute buckets, and (5) emits lines like <tenant>/:undefined:. Logging to Time-Series Pipeline

flowchart TD A[Logs Generated] --> B[Rotation by hour] B --> C[Read /logs/tenants/*/services/**] C --> D[Decompress *.log.gz via zcat] C --> E[Parse lines: text or JSON] D --> F E --> F[Extract tenant/service, level] F --> G[Filter: ERROR] G --> H[Window: last 10 minutes] H --> I[Group: tenant/service] I --> J[Output: /::] J --> K[Console / Pipeline to storage]

Did you know? Some of the largest observability stacks process millions of log lines per second across dozens of regions, challenging even modern time-series backends.

Key Takeaways

  • Use a sliding 10-minute window for near-real-time counts.
  • Parse both text and JSON log formats robustly.
  • Skip unreadable files and gz archives without breaking the stream.

Wrapping Up

The journey began with Uber’s need for scalable observability and culminates in a practical pattern: treat log analytics as a streaming problem, leverage robust backends when scale demands it, and keep the pipeline simple at its core. Tomorrow’s teams can start small, then grow the pipeline as the data and users expand, always prioritizing per-tenant, per-service visibility.
