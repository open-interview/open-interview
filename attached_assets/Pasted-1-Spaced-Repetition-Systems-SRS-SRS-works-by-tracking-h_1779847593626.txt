1. Spaced Repetition Systems (SRS)SRS works by tracking how easy or difficult a flashcard is for you. Harder cards are shown frequently, while easy cards are pushed far into the future, dramatically dropping study times.The Core Mechanism: Review intervals typically expand exponentially (e.g., Day 1 \(\rightarrow \) Day 3 \(\rightarrow \) Day 7 \(\rightarrow \) Day 21 \(\rightarrow \) Day 45).Popular Software Tools:Anki: The most powerful, open-source flashcard tool supporting custom algorithms and media.RemNote: A note-taking app that automatically turns your outlines into SRS flashcards.Mochi: A clean, markdown-based flashcard app with built-in SRS mapping.2. Active Recall (The Fuel for SRS)SRS is highly ineffective if you only passive-read your flashcards. You must pair it with Active Recall—forcing your brain to retrieve information without looking at the answer.The "Muscle" Effect: Simulating memory retrieval trains the brain to build stronger neural pathways.How to use it: When using SRS, look at a prompt, form the answer completely in your mind (or speak it aloud), and then flip the card.3. The Feynman Technique (For Deep Understanding)You cannot efficiently memorize what you do not understand. The Feynman Technique ensures you grasp complex concepts before putting them into your SRS decks.Step 1: Choose a concept you want to study.Step 2: Explain it simply on a blank sheet of paper as if teaching it to an 11-year-old.Step 3: Identify gaps in your explanation where you used complex jargon or struggled.Step 4: Return to your source material to fix those gaps, then simplify further.4. The Memory Palace (Method of Loci)For data that lacks narrative structure (such as long lists, chemical structures, or legal articles), the Memory Palace uses your brain’s spatial memory.How it works: Mentalize a familiar physical space, like your childhood home or school.Anchoring: Anchor vivid, bizarre mental images representing your data points to specific furniture or rooms along a path.SRS Integration: Create an SRS card that prompts you to mentally walk through that specific palace path.5. Advanced Card Creation RulesThe quality of your learning depends heavily on how you write your SRS flashcards. Follow these rules for maximum retention:The Minimum Information Principle: Keep cards incredibly simple. A card should ask for exactly one atomic piece of information.Context Over Isolation: When learning foreign languages, learn full example sentences instead of isolated vocabulary words.Cloze Deletions: Use fill-in-the-blank cards (e.g., "The capital of Australia is [...]"). This focuses your attention strictly on the missing variable.
