> **Picture this:** It was a real-world moment that jolts a team awake: Stripe Radar confronted a surge of card-testing attacks and needed an ultra-low-latency risk score that could run on streaming signals, all while evolving with attackers’ tricks [1]. That pressure isn’t unique to payments—any streaming micro-event environment can collapse under a model that isn’t built for real-time constraints. The takeaway is clear: latency budgets don’t merely constrain engineering; they shape the very design of the detector that stands between user experience and fraud. You’ll walk through how to design a CPU-friendly baseline that starts with a narrow 40-feature view and scales through online updates, drift detection, and careful calibration, all while keeping inference under 200 ms per event.

## Hooking the reader to a real world constraint

Building on Stripe’s experience with radar-like real-time risk scoring, this article frames a practical journey: a beginner-friendly baseline that can run on commodity CPUs, handling 1k labeled anomalies across 40 features with a strict ≤ 200 ms per-event latency target. The stakes are tangible: a single milliseconds-long delay can translate into missed fraud signals or frustrated users. In the Stripe case, latency budgets drove the design toward streaming, feature-store-backed inference and layered signals that adapt quickly to evolving attack patterns [1]. Consequently, the first decision is to align feature engineering and model choice with the CPU and latency constraints, not the other way around. In parallel, consider the necessity of online updates and drift monitoring to avoid stagnation in a shifting threat landscape [2][3].

## The architecture you’ll build

This approach centers on a feature-store-driven inference path with a small, CPU-friendly model set. Specifically, 40 normalized features feed a choice between logistic regression (L2 regularization) and a shallow tree, complemented by an unsupervised isolation forest for a robust, multi-signal baseline. The system is designed for streaming batches (e.g., 1k events per batch) where online learning happens via partial_fit on the labeled portion and lightweight updates on the unsupervised path. The architecture benefits from a modular data plane and a lean control plane that guards latency while enabling quick retraining when drift is detected [4][5].

## What to run on CPU—models and preprocessing

Preprocessing focuses on consistent 40-feature normalization and minimal feature transformations that preserve information without heavy computation. The baseline evaluates: 
- Logistic Regression (L2, C = 1e-4) for a fast, linear decision boundary; 
- A small depth-4 decision tree for nonlinear decisions with bounded compute; 
- An unsupervised Isolation Forest operating in parallel to capture unlabeled anomalies and provide a robust counterpoint to supervised signals. For streaming, use partial_fit on batches of 1k events to keep memory and compute predictable while maintaining online adaptability [3][6].

## Drift, calibration, and online updates

Concept drift is the core challenge in real-time fraud detection: attacker patterns shift faster than static models can adapt. The plan includes lightweight drift detectors and calibration steps to align predicted scores with observed frequencies, plus an online update cadence that retrains on a rolling window of recent events and occasional supervised recalls when labels arrive. The aim is a pragmatic guardrail: quick adaptation without destabilizing the critical path. This aligns with general drift literature and practical software patterns for streaming ML in production [2][5].

## Data splits and evaluation in a streaming world

Instead of traditional train/test splits, use time-based windows and rolling evaluation: 
- Train on historical window, validate on the immediate next window, and score on the live stream; 
- Use metrics that reflect anomaly detection goals: AUROC for ranking, precision/recall at target false-positive rates, and calibration curves for thresholding stability; 
- Track latency as a primary metric, ensuring the system stays under the 200 ms budget per event on CPU; 
- Monitor drifting distributions and trigger retraining when drift indicators cross thresholds. This approach mirrors how real-time risk systems balance detection quality with operational constraints [4][6].

## A real-world proof of concept

The practical pattern here mirrors Stripe’s real-world emphasis: embed ML libraries in the critical path, rely on a feature-store for online inference, and layer evolving signals to outpace attackers. This isn’t just theory—the approach echoes how teams build guardrails around continuous model releases to preserve effectiveness without harming user experience [1]. The takeaway is to treat latency budgets as design constraints that drive the entire pipeline, from feature engineering to model selection and update cadence [1][7].
