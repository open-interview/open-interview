> **Picture this:** It was a night when a busy Unix host started screaming in silence. Netflix’s Dynomite project faced a production race where rapidly growing daemon logs weren’t safely rotated, threatening a cascade of open-file descriptor leaks and a ‘Too many open files’ crisis [1]. This real-world scare isn’t just about logs; it’s about the clockwork of processes holding onto resources just a heartbeat too long. The episode became a blueprint for teams learning to design rotations that don’t assume grace periods, but enforce reopening and cleanup as a guardrail. [1]

## Hooked by a real-world wake‑up call

Many developers discover that log rotation is more myth than magic until a production lull reveals the fragility. Netflix’s Dynomite issue demonstrates this vividly: if processes keep fds open to rotated logs, filesystem cleanup can stall and the system can hit an operational wall [1]. Building on this, the problem becomes a practical exercise in resource accounting rather than a theoretical exercise in file gymnastics. The lesson is simple but powerful: rotation must be paired with a strategy to close or reopen fds, not just rename files. 

For context, the /proc filesystem exposes per-process state including open file descriptors, which is how one can audit who’s still holding onto what during rotation [2][3]. This is not vanity data; it’s a map of who’s using what right now, and where the leverage points lie in a running system. [2][3]

## The hunt for open descriptors under /logs/app

To identify the likely offenders, a robust one-liner would need to count, per PID, how many file descriptors reference any path under /logs/app (both .log and .log.gz). The approach hinges on resolving each fd via readlink, filtering by path prefix, and then sorting to surface the top offenders. The key insight is that each process may hold multiple references, and some descriptors may be inaccessible or already closed; robust logic must skip those safely [2][3]. A practical, self-contained command—relying only on standard UNIX tools—embodies this principle and yields a ranked list of PIDs by open-log references. Consider the core idea as a filter map: iterate PIDs → enumerate fds → resolve paths → count matches → sort by count. The mechanism aligns with how teams reason about leak risks in production systems [4].

## A twist in the tale: edge cases matter

The journey reveals counterintuitive edges: some descriptors point to transient or deleted files, some paths resolve to rotated names, and a few /proc entries may be inaccessible due to permissions or kernel state. The robust approach must gracefully skip inaccessible /proc entries, ignore non-log paths, and still output meaningful top offenders. This is not just an exercise in scripting; it’s a discipline in defensive operations—anticipating race conditions between log producers and rotation—an issue Netflix highlighted in their battle with runaway logs [1]. Additionally, best practices around logs—such as ensuring rotation strategies that safely reopen or truncate files (for example, copytruncate-style approaches in containers)—help prevent this entire class of leak from ever forming [4].

## Proof from the field: Netflix’s lesson, translated into practice

The core takeaway is actionable: verify open deleted files and confirm that rotation does not leave fds dangling. Netflix’s experience shows the stakes: a loosely coupled rotation can degrade service reliability under load, potentially triggering systemic failures rather than granting a graceful decay. The public issue document recounts the risk pattern and reinforces how to align rotation with a reopen/truncate strategy to mitigate leaks in dynamic, containerized environments [1][4]. In short, the right checks turn a ticking clock into a predictable, bounded risk.

> 💡 **Did you know?** Some production teams discovered that a single lingering fd in a daemonic process could cascade into hours of degraded throughput—even when most workers were healthy.

## Wrapping Up

Log rotation isn’t a passive operation; it’s a resource-management discipline. By tying rotation to explicit closure or reopening of fds and by verifying open-deleted-file leaks, teams turn a potential disaster into a predictable, auditable process. The Netflix case serves as a reminder that the smallest leaks can ripple into outages; the discipline is the antidote.
